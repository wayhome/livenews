Frank Herbert’s *Dune* (1965) draws inspiration from the story of Lawrence of Arabia, particularly in the character of Paul Atreides, who, like T.E. Lawrence, is a foreign figure immersing himself in a desert culture to lead a revolt against oppressors. The 1962 film *Lawrence of Arabia* presented a romanticized version of Lawrence as a "white savior," a theme that resonated with Western audiences during a period of complex U.S. relations in the Middle East and Cold War tensions. Herbert, however, adapted and modified Lawrence’s story, creating a nuanced narrative that both echoes and contrasts with the historical account. While *Dune* shares similarities with Lawrence’s experiences as documented in *Seven Pillars of Wisdom*, such as cultural immersion and leadership, it also diverges by exploring themes of messianic leadership, manipulation of cultures, and differing portrayals of women and religion. Critics debate whether *Dune* perpetuates the "white savior" narrative, a topic that remains contentious in discussions of Herbert’s work.
